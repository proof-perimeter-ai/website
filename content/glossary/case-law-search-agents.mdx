Case law search agents are AI systems that conduct legal research the way an associate would — but across the entire corpus of judicial decisions: given a legal question or fact pattern, the agent formulates searches, reads candidate cases, assesses their relevance and precedential weight, follows citation trails to earlier authority, checks whether cases remain good law, and synthesizes findings into a memo with pinpoint citations. The agentic structure matters because legal research is inherently iterative — each case read suggests new search terms, distinguishing facts, and lines of authority the initial query never anticipated.

The underlying machinery combines legal-domain retrieval with language-model reasoning. Semantic search finds conceptually relevant decisions beyond keyword matches; citation-graph analysis surfaces the authoritative cases a topic orbits; and the model's reading distinguishes holding from dicta, majority from dissent, and binding from persuasive authority given the jurisdiction. The non-negotiable engineering requirement is verifiability: every proposition must carry a real citation to a real case, with the quoted language actually present at the cited location — the documented failures of lawyers filing briefs with hallucinated cases made citation grounding the defining quality bar for legal AI.

Used well, these agents change the economics of thoroughness rather than replacing judgment. Research that justified four associate-hours now supports a first-pass answer in minutes, which means smaller matters get research depth previously reserved for large ones, and experienced lawyers spend their time evaluating the agent's synthesis — is this line of authority really analogous? what would opposing counsel cite? — rather than assembling it. Professional responsibility stays with the lawyer, which is why the agent's work product is structured for verification: claims linked to sources, search coverage disclosed, and uncertainty flagged instead of papered over.
